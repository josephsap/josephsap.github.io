Jamba Juice App

Roles: Prototyping/UI/UX/Strategy

Our challenge:

During our first hour of school at BDW, Neighbor Agency delivered a brief from their client, Jamba Juice. They needed a mobile app that reduces menu confusion and in-store wait time, which are two big problems facing customers and franchisees.

Research:

My group of three visited Jamba Juice stores, researched mobile apps, and questioned friends and family. We decided to go with a mood-based theme, concluding that consumers order based on a combination of how they are feeling and their taste preferences.

Prototyping:

Throughout the two weeks, we went through plenty of sketching, deliberation, and compromising. We made paper prototypes, wireframes, and used online prototyping tools. Throughout the process, we iterated over and over again. At the end of the two weeks the project culminated with a presentation. Neighbor Agency presented all of the designs to the client, and our concept was chosen as their favorite.

Spider App

Medicine + The Military

Role: Lead Front-End Development, UX, Visual Design, Workflow Optimization

The Military is having trouble recruiting doctors, so my team is building an informative website to clarify the application process, drive interest, dispel myths, and ultimately increase the number of doctors joining the military.

This was a project where I was able to show that I'm more than a developer. Once I was informed that I would be involved, I immediately insisted that the six key people building the site (me, UX, art director, designer, content, copy) work together early on and frequently. We did, and the result was a happy and impressed client and account team. We were able to show how a site's theme can be carried out through interaction design, and I created a demo to further illustrate the point to the client.

I made many development decisions too. Since the timeline and budget were short, I chose to use Bootstrap to speed things up on my end. Content will be added to the site in the future, so the backend developer and I chose to use Drupal to make it easy for a copywriter to update the content without a developer's help. Since our primary target audience is college-age and tech-savvy, we chose to carry over mobile icons and interactions to the desktop (see the menu).

The menu opens and closes on scroll.

The UX designer and I also put together a round of testing using Mullen interns as subjects (they were part of our target audience) using paper prototypes of the site.

Every animation is built with speed in mind. I'm animating properties that are easy for the browser to process, such as using translate instead of animating the top position.

Technologies used: JavaScript, jQuery, SCSS, Bootstrap, Grunt.js, Modernizr, Drupal, Icon fonts

Today's Military Website

Role: Development

One of Mullen's clients is the Department of Defense, and I helped develop a responsive site to inform someone considering joining the military of their options. I came in for the last five months of two-year process, taking over for another developer. I was responsible for the majority of the responsive CSS, the map implementation, and many JavaScript and CMS tasks.

The main challenge for this project was re-writing many lines of CSS. I felt the site was built incorrectly, so I had to re-do a large amount of work. Why?

For example, in this section, with an image to the left and text to the right, the original developer placed the text to the right with padding, which on smaller screens, resulted in text wrapping below the image. I re-wrote all of these instances and floated each section to the left and right. In the process, I was also able to cut out many lines of code.

Another significant task was implementing the interactive map of all the military bases around the world. (Click the image to see it on the live site.)

This was another re-write, requiring me to remove 97% of the original code because it was crashing on iOS 7 and in IE8. I became quite familiar with the Google Maps docs and working with JavaScript objects.

Technologies used: JavaScript, jQuery, SCSS, Google Maps API, Drupal, PHP

Seventh Generation Baby Photobomber

Role: Build a JavaScript Prototype

Inspired by unbaby.me, Seventh Generation asked Made Movement to create a Facebook app where users could bomb their friend's photos with images of a baby. I was asked to create a prototype of the masking feature which output the final coordinates and would "bounce" the image back if the user tried to position it outisde of the clipping mask.

Process:

I created a "clipping mask" in PhotoShop then placed an image behind it. Since I had to get this project completed in a limited amount of time, I used jQuery UI's draggable feature, then added my own JavaScript to it. The final coordinates are output to the console, and the image automatically bounces back to cover the whole mask if it's out of bounds.

piq, a jukebox for social spaces

Roles: Development/UX/Copywriting/Business Development

The second semester at BDW is called Startup, which is essentially an accelerator like TechStars. My team, piq, made a "jukebox". It is a hardeware device that plugs in to your speakers, and since it has WiFi, users can pull up the web app and choose what song plays next, creating a collaborative playlist. The prototype was built on a Raspberry Pi and the music comes from Spotify.

My Role:

I did whatever was needed including building and designing the marketing site, writing and editing, coordinating a Kickstarter video shoot, and plenty of user testing and research.

Taking something from concept to reality was in invaluable experience. Learning where to spend your time and what to do next were the major takeaways. There's no doubt this experience will benefit me whenever I have to work in a team.

Design Case Study:

Above is an early mock for the app's user interface. It features two sliders that default to display five songs and five artists. The image on the right is my design which defaults to a long scrolling list of songs, artists, or albums. We had users searching for songs and wondering why they were only getting five results. My design was passed over in favor of the sliding design, which I felt wasn't intuitive. My design was eventually validated when one of our design teachers sketched my concept without having seen it. What did I learn? Next time, I have to push harder for my design, back it up with research, and show my reasoning.

Outcome:

The team won PitchFest, which was the semester-ending competition amongst the six BDW Startup teams. The Kickstarter campaign fell well short of its $55,000 goal.

  • Concepting: Nike World Cup 2014

    Roles: Concepting, presentation, user stories

    Wirestone Group tasked my BDW class team with a challenge: Create a World Cup experience worthy of Nike.

    Keeping in mind soccer fans' team loyalty and the fact that not everyone will be able to travel to Brazil for the World Cup, we created a team-centric experience that anyone, anywhere could participate in. The first part is the app that changes your phone to your team's colors, gives you team-related content, and helps you find where your fellow fans are watching the game. Of course, you can buy Nike product right in the app.

    If a fan is in Brazil watching the matches, they can interact with the vantage wall using the Kinect. If you stand in front of the wall with the app on, your body would show up and act like a clipping mask, offering up team-related content. It's interactive, meaning you can see past World Cup highlights, get stats, and see what's happening on social media. Then, if you stand close enough to a fan of a different team, the overlapping content would show the history between both teams. Nike product would be integrated as a timeline so you could view the soccer shoes' evolution since its inception.
